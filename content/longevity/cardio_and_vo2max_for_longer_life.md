# Cardio & VO₂max for a Longer, Better Life

## 1. Why cardio fitness matters so much

Cardiorespiratory fitness is one of the strongest single predictors of:

- All-cause mortality (risk of dying from any cause)
- Cardiovascular events (heart attack, stroke)
- Functional ability in later life
- Everyday energy, mood, and resilience

In BioFlo we treat cardio fitness as:

> "How powerful and efficient your heart–lung–muscle system is when you move."

Two big pieces:

- **Base** – how long you can cruise at a steady, sustainable effort.
- **Peak** – how hard you can go when you really push yourself.

Those map to:

- **Zone 2 training** – building the base (engine efficiency).
- **VO₂max-style intervals** – raising the peak (engine horsepower).

---

## 2. VO₂max in plain English

**VO₂max** is the maximum rate at which your body can use oxygen during intense exercise.

It reflects:

- Heart strength and stroke volume  
- Lung capacity and gas exchange  
- Blood flow and oxygen carrying capacity  
- Muscles' ability to extract and use oxygen  

Higher VO₂max is consistently associated with:

- Lower risk of early death
- Lower risk of heart disease and metabolic disease
- Better tolerance of illness, surgery, and stressful events

We care about:

- Your starting point
- How it changes over time
- Roughly where you sit versus age/sex norms (percentiles, not perfection)

---

## 3. How VO₂max is measured or estimated

### 3.1. Lab test

- Performed on a bike or treadmill while you wear a mask.
- Intensity ramps up until you hit your sustainable limit.
- Outputs a VO₂max value in ml/kg/min.

Pros:

- Most accurate snapshot.

Cons:

- Not everyone has access or wants to do this regularly.

### 3.2. Field test (e.g., 12-minute run/walk)

- Warm up.
- Cover as much distance as you can in 12 minutes on a flat route or track.
- Use a standard formula to estimate VO₂max from distance.

This is not perfect, but it's:

- Free
- Repeatable
- Great for tracking change over time

### 3.3. Wearable estimates

- Many devices estimate VO₂max from heart rate and pace data.
- Treat these as **rough trends**, not exact truth.
- They become more meaningful if you:
  - Train regularly
  - Include some hard efforts

## 4. Zone 2 – the aerobic base builder

Zone 2 is a steady effort where:

- You can still talk in short sentences.
- Breathing is noticeably heavier but under control.
- You feel like you're working, but you could continue for a while.

Rough rules:

- Too easy = you can chat freely, almost no effort → probably Zone 1.
- Too hard = you can barely get words out → you've drifted into higher zones.

Zone 2 is powerful because it:

- Improves heart and blood vessel health.
- Increases mitochondrial function in muscle cells.
- Enhances fat oxidation and metabolic flexibility.
- Supports insulin sensitivity and blood sugar control.
- Allows high training volume with relatively low injury risk.

### 4.1. How much Zone 2?

Typical longevity-oriented patterns:

- Target around **2–5 hours per week**.
- A practical sweet spot for many people is **~3 hours/week**, for example:
  - 3 × 60 minutes, or
  - 4–5 × 30–45 minutes

If you're just starting:

- Begin with 2–3 × 20 minutes
- Increase duration slowly as your conditioning and joints adapt

### 4.2. What counts as Zone 2?

Examples:

- Brisk walking (especially with incline)
- Cycling (indoor or outdoor)
- Rowing
- Elliptical
- Swimming

Stop–start sports (tennis, football, etc.) are great for overall activity, but heart rate tends to bounce across zones. BioFlo will still encourage a **separate, structured Zone 2 block** most weeks.

## 5. VO₂max training – raising the ceiling

Once you have some weeks of consistent Zone 2 under your belt, you can sprinkle in **high-intensity intervals** that specifically push VO₂max.

Typical structure:

- Intervals of **3–8 minutes** at a hard, sustained effort  
- Equal-duration recovery between intervals (1:1 work:rest)

Example:

- 10–15 minutes easy warm-up
- 4 × 4 minutes hard + 4 minutes easy
- 5–10 minutes easy cool-down

During the hard blocks you should feel:

- Very challenged
- Unable to speak more than a few words
- But still in control of technique and breathing (not panic level)

### 5.1. How often?

A common pattern:

- ~**80% of your cardio time** = easy/Zone 2
- ~**20%** = higher-intensity VO₂max work

For many people:

- 2–3 Zone 2 sessions/week
- 1 VO₂max-style interval session/week

More is *not* always better. VO₂max work is stressful; quality and recovery matter.

## 6. Cardio + strength + stability = resilient aging

Cardio training is crucial, but it doesn't stand alone.

For a robust "marginal decade," you also need:

- **Strength**
  - Push (push-ups/presses)
  - Pull (rows, pull-ups)
  - Hinge (deadlifts, hip hinges)
  - Squat (squats, step-ups)
  - Carry (farmers carries, suitcases)

- **Stability**
  - Balance (single-leg work, eyes-closed balance)
  - Trunk control (dead bugs, Pallof presses, anti-rotation)
  - Joint control through full ranges of motion

BioFlo's stance:

> "You want a strong engine, a strong chassis, and good suspension."

We'll encourage:

- 2–3 strength sessions per week  
- Stability woven into warm-ups and accessory work  
- Cardio layered around that in a way you can actually sustain

## 7. Safety and medical guardrails

Cardio training is usually beneficial, but there are important safety boundaries.

BioFlo Coach will:

- Encourage gradual progression, not sudden extremes.
- Emphasise warm-up, technique, and listening to warning signs.
- Flag situations where you should talk to your doctor, for example:
  - Unexplained chest pain or pressure.
  - Dizziness, fainting, or severe breathlessness with light effort.
  - Known heart disease or serious conditions and uncertainty about exercise safety.

The coach will **never**:

- Clear you for intense training if you report red-flag symptoms.
- Tell you which cardiac medications to take.
- Replace professional medical evaluation.

All cardio guidance is educational and behavioural in nature, and should be adapted together with your own healthcare team if you have significant medical history.
